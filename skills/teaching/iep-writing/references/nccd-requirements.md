# NCCD Requirements for Victorian Schools

## Overview

The Nationally Consistent Collection of Data on School Students with Disability (NCCD) is an annual collection that counts the number of school students receiving adjustments due to disability, and the level of adjustment provided. It is mandatory for all Australian schools and directly affects funding.

Source: Australian Government Department of Education, NCCD Portal — https://www.nccd.edu.au/

## NCCD Categories

Students are counted in one of four disability categories:

| Category | Description |
|---|---|
| **Physical** | Conditions affecting mobility, motor control, or physical health (e.g., cerebral palsy, spina bifida, chronic fatigue) |
| **Cognitive** | Conditions affecting intellectual functioning, memory, or problem-solving (e.g., intellectual disability, specific learning disorder, acquired brain injury) |
| **Sensory** | Conditions affecting vision, hearing, or sensory processing (e.g., blindness, deafness, sensory processing disorder) |
| **Social/Emotional** | Conditions affecting social interaction, emotional regulation, or mental health (e.g., autism spectrum disorder, anxiety disorder, ADHD, oppositional defiant disorder) |

A student is included under NCCD if they have a disability as defined by the Disability Discrimination Act 1992 and the Disability Standards for Education 2005, AND the school provides adjustments to address their functional impact.

## Levels of Adjustment

The level is determined by the **adjustments provided**, not the student's diagnosis:

| Level | Description | Evidence threshold |
|---|---|---|
| **Quality Differentiated Teaching Practice (QDTP)** | Adjustments within regular classroom practice that a teacher makes for all students (e.g., differentiated instruction, varied assessment methods) | Minimal — teacher records of differentiation |
| **Supplementary** | Additional adjustments beyond QDTP, provided at some times during the week (e.g., small group literacy intervention 3×/week, modified worksheets, visual schedule, occasional teacher aide support) | Moderate — timetabled interventions, adjusted tasks, meeting notes |
| **Substantial** | Frequent, significant adjustments provided most days (e.g., regular 1:1 teacher aide support, highly modified curriculum across multiple subjects, frequent breaks/withdrawal, daily behaviour support) | Detailed — IEP/BSP, timetable showing support hours, modified curriculum documents, specialist reports |
| **Extensive** | Comprehensive, constant, individualised adjustments provided at all times (e.g., full-time aide, completely individualised program, intensive behaviour support, communication device use throughout the day) | Comprehensive — full IEP, specialist assessments, timetables showing constant 1:1, detailed daily records |

## The 10-Week Evidence Rule

Adjustments must have been provided for a **minimum of 10 weeks** of school in the 12 months preceding the NCCD census date (usually August). This doesn't mean the student must have been at the school for 10 weeks — it means the adjustments have been in place for at least 10 weeks.

## What Counts as Evidence

Evidence must show:
1. **The student has a disability** (diagnosis, specialist report, or school-observed functional impact)
2. **Adjustments are being provided** (documentation of what, when, how often, by whom)
3. **The adjustments are monitored and reviewed** (data collection, plan reviews, adjustments modified based on progress)

### Acceptable evidence types

- IEPs, ILPs, BSPs (dated, implemented, reviewed)
- Timetables showing withdrawal groups, aide support, or therapy sessions
- Annotated work samples showing adjustments
- Meeting minutes (SSG meetings, parent meetings, specialist consultations)
- Professional learning records (training for specific adjustments)
- Therapy reports and recommendations
- Records of communication with specialists
- Assessment data showing progress (or lack thereof — justifying continued adjustment)
- Daily behaviour charts, check-in/check-out data
- Incident reports (for behaviour-related adjustments)

### The school's NCCD evidence should tell a story

> "This student has [diagnosis/functional need]. Here is how it impacts their learning. These are the adjustments we provide. This is how often and by whom. Here is evidence the adjustments are being delivered. Here is how we review and modify them. Here is what has changed as a result."

## NCCD and IEP Documentation

When writing an IEP that will serve as NCCD evidence:

- State the NCCD category and level of adjustment explicitly
- Reference the 10-week timeline
- Document the frequency and duration of adjustments (not just their existence)
- Include review dates that show ongoing monitoring
- Retain annotated evidence that shows adjustments in action
- Cross-reference other evidence sources (specialist reports, meeting minutes)

## Common Documentation Gaps (what auditors look for)

1. **Adjustments listed but not evidenced** — "Small group support 3×/week" is listed in the IEP, but there are no records showing it actually happened
2. **Adjustments unchanged across plans** — If a student's adjustments are identical across 12 months of IEPs, it suggests the plan isn't being meaningfully reviewed
3. **No student voice** — The student's perspective is absent
4. **Adjustments described vaguely** — "Additional support" says nothing; "Teacher aide support for 30 mins daily during literacy, focusing on decoding using the school's phonics intervention program" is evidence
5. **No link to curriculum** — Adjustments aren't connected to what the student is actually learning

## Key Dates

- NCCD census date: First Friday in August each year
- Schools submit data through the NCCD Portal
- Funding follows the NCCD data — accurate documentation = accurate funding
