# Report Card Writing (Victorian Schools)

This skill helps VIT-registered teachers write professional, curriculum-aligned student report card comments that comply with Victorian Government school reporting requirements. It produces parent-friendly comments structured around achievement, growth, and strategies for improvement.

## Before You Begin

Check what the teacher has provided and ask only for what's missing:

1. **Student context** — Name, preferred pronouns, year level
2. **Subject/learning area** — Which subject is this report for?
3. **Achievement level** — Where is the student on the 5-point scale?
4. **Evidence** — What observations, assessments, or work samples inform this comment?
5. **School type** — Government, Catholic, or independent? (government schools have specific DET requirements)
6. **Reporting period** — Semester 1, Semester 2, or progress report?

If the teacher hasn't specified the student's achievement level against the Victorian Curriculum, ask. The comment must be anchored to the achievement standard.

## The Victorian Reporting Framework

### The 5-Point Scale

Victorian government schools must use an age-related 5-point scale for English, Mathematics, and Science. For all other subjects, schools may use the 5-point scale based on learning goals or dimensions.

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Well above** | Consistently demonstrates the achievement standard well above the level expected for their age |
| **Above** | Demonstrates the achievement standard above the level expected for their age |
| **At** | Demonstrates the achievement standard at the level expected for their age |
| **Below** | Working towards the achievement standard expected for their age |
| **Well below** | Requires significant support to access the achievement standard expected for their age |

### English Reporting — By Mode

English must be reported across three modes (not a single English score):
- **Reading and Viewing**
- **Writing**
- **Speaking and Listening**

Each mode gets its own 5-point scale rating and comment. A student may be At standard in Speaking and Listening but Below in Writing — that's normal, and the comments should reflect those distinct profiles.

### Mathematics Reporting — Single Aggregated Score

From 2025, Mathematics Version 2.0 uses a **single aggregated score** against the achievement standard as a whole (not separate strands). The first report under Version 2.0 shows achievement only, no progress line, because v1.0 and v2.0 are not directly comparable.

### Minimum Reporting Frequency

| Learning Area | Minimum |
|---|---|
| English | Twice yearly |
| Mathematics | Twice yearly |
| Science | At least once yearly (from Year 3) |
| Physical Education | Twice yearly |
| All other areas | At least biennially |

### Plain English Requirement

Reports must be in **plain English** accessible to parents and carers. This means:
- Short sentences
- Everyday words (not "utilises metacognitive strategies" → say "thinks about their own learning")
- Active voice ("Sarah writes clear paragraphs" not "Clear paragraphs are written by Sarah")
- No unexplained acronyms (write out "Individual Education Plan (IEP)" on first use)
- Culturally sensitive language

## Workflow

### Step 1: Anchor to the Achievement Standard

For **F–10**: Read `shared-references/victorian-curriculum-f10.md`.
For **VCE**: Read `shared-references/vce-study-designs.md`.

The comment must reference what the student *can* do in relation to the achievement standard for their year level. The 5-point scale measures against age-expected standards, not against effort or improvement alone.

### Step 2: Structure the Comment

Every report comment should follow this three-part structure:

```
[Achievement statement] — what the student can do, referencing the curriculum
[Area for growth] — a specific, actionable next step
[Strategy for home] — something parents/carers can do to support
```

### Step 3: Write Using the Framework

```markdown
## Report Comment Structure

### 1. Achievement Statement (2–3 sentences)
Begin with a positive, specific observation about what the student has achieved or demonstrated. Reference curriculum content where possible. Use the student's name. Be concrete — not "[Name] is doing well" but "[Name] can identify the main idea in a text and explain how the author supports it with evidence."

### 2. Area for Growth (1–2 sentences)
Identify one specific next step. This is not a negative — it's a learning pathway. Phrase it forward-looking: "[Name]'s next step is to..." or "[Name] is learning to..." Avoid: "needs to," "struggles with," "cannot yet."

### 3. Strategies for Home (1–2 sentences)
Suggest one or two things parents/carers can do at home. Make them practical, free, and part of daily life — not "buy a workbook." Best strategies: "When reading together, ask..." or "Encourage [Name] to explain their thinking by saying 'tell me how you worked that out.'"
```

### Step 4: Calibrate by Achievement Level

The tone and focus shifts depending on the student's level:

**Well above standard:**
- Acknowledge advanced understanding without creating pressure
- Focus on deepening and broadening, not accelerating to next year's content
- Strategy: "Encourage [Name] to explain their thinking to others — this deepens understanding"

**Above standard:**
- Acknowledge strong performance with specifics
- Focus on one area where they can extend
- Strategy: "[Name] would benefit from opportunities to apply this skill in new contexts"

**At standard:**
- Affirm that meeting the standard is genuine achievement
- Identify the natural next step in the learning progression
- Strategy: "When [Name] finishes a task, ask them to show you how they know their answer is correct"

**Below standard:**
- Acknowledge effort and progress, be honest but encouraging
- Focus on one achievable step — not everything at once
- Strategy: "At home, short daily practice of [specific skill] will help build confidence"
- Important: If the student has an IEP/ILP, reference the goals from that plan. Their progress should be measured against their individual goals, not just the age-expected standard.

**Well below standard:**
- Be honest but compassionate
- Reference the student's individual learning plan if one exists
- Focus on what they CAN do and the very next step
- Avoid deficit language — frame challenges as areas the school is actively supporting
- Strategy: "We are working on [specific skill] at school. At home, [simple daily practice]"

### Step 5: Quality Check

Before finalising, review each comment for:

1. **Did you name the student?** Yes, use their name. It personalises the comment.
2. **Is it in plain English?** Would a parent with no education background understand every sentence?
3. **Is it specific?** "Doing well" says nothing. "Can construct a scientific argument using data as evidence" says something.
4. **Is there a next step?** Every comment needs a forward-looking element — that's how reports support learning.
5. **Is the tone right?** Reports are formal but warm. They should sound like a caring professional, not a bureaucrat.
6. **Is the pronoun correct?** Use the student's preferred pronouns consistently.
7. **No surprises rule**: If a student is significantly below standard, this should not be the first time parents are hearing about it. Flag this to the teacher if it seems like new information.

## Important Principles

### Why plain English matters
Victorian schools serve diverse communities. Parents may speak languages other than English at home, may have had negative school experiences themselves, or may not understand educational terminology. Plain English is inclusive and respectful.

### Why the 5-point scale is age-related, not effort-based
The scale measures achievement against the Victorian Curriculum standard for the student's age. A student working very hard but achieving below the standard receives a "Below" or "Well below" — not because they're not trying, but because the report communicates where they are against the curriculum. The comment should acknowledge effort separately.

### Why three parts, not just one
A comment that only describes achievement gives parents a snapshot but no direction. A comment that only lists deficits is demoralising. The three-part structure (achievement + growth + strategy) respects the student, informs the parent, and supports learning.

### Handling sensitive situations
- If a student has made limited progress, be honest but focus on the path forward
- If there are behavioural or social concerns, frame as skills to develop, not character flaws
- If a student is working on an adjusted curriculum (IEP/ILP), the comment should reference their individual goals, not the age-expected standard
- Never use report comments to raise new significant concerns — these should have been communicated earlier
