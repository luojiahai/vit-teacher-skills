# Report Comment Frameworks

## Overview

This reference provides practical frameworks and language patterns for writing report comments across the 5-point scale. Use these as starting points, adapting to the specific student, subject, and achievement level.

## The Three-Part Comment Structure

Every report comment should follow this structure:

### Part 1: Achievement Statement (what the student can do)
- Describe what the student has achieved, linked to the Victorian Curriculum
- Be specific — reference skills, understandings, or behaviours you've observed
- This is where you locate the student against the achievement standard

### Part 2: Area for Growth (the next step)
- Identify one specific next step in the learning progression
- Phrase positively — this is a learning pathway, not a deficit
- Avoid: "cannot," "struggles with," "needs to" → prefer: "is learning to," "next step is," "ready to"

### Part 3: Strategies for Home (how parents can help)
- Suggest 1–2 practical, free, everyday activities
- Make them part of normal family life, not extra homework
- Best strategies involve conversation, reading together, or everyday maths

## Language by Achievement Level

### Well Above Standard

**Achievement language patterns**:
- "[Name] consistently demonstrates sophisticated understanding of..."
- "[Name] independently applies... across a range of contexts"
- "[Name]'s ability to... is well above the expected level for [Year X]"

**Growth language patterns**:
- "[Name] is ready to explore... at greater depth"
- "[Name] would benefit from opportunities to explain their thinking and reasoning"
- "To extend [Name] further, we are..."

**Home strategies**:
- "When reading together, ask [Name] to evaluate the author's choices rather than just summarise"
- "Encourage [Name] to teach you a concept they've learned — explaining to others deepens understanding"
- "Provide opportunities for [Name] to apply their skills to real-world problems"

### Above Standard

**Achievement language patterns**:
- "[Name] demonstrates a strong understanding of..."
- "[Name] can confidently... and is beginning to..."
- "[Name]'s skills in... are above the expected level for [Year X]"

**Growth language patterns**:
- "[Name]'s next step is to refine..."
- "[Name] is developing the ability to..."
- "With continued practice, [Name] will..."

**Home strategies**:
- "Ask [Name] to show you how they worked something out — the explanation builds deeper understanding"
- "Provide opportunities for [Name] to apply these skills in different situations"

### At Standard

**Achievement language patterns**:
- "[Name] can... which is at the expected standard for [Year X]"
- "[Name] demonstrates the ability to... consistently"
- "[Name] has made pleasing progress in..."

**Growth language patterns**:
- "[Name] is now working on..."
- "[Name]'s next step is to..."
- "[Name] is ready to extend their learning by..."

**Home strategies**:
- "When [Name] finishes a task, ask them to explain their approach — this builds mathematical reasoning"
- "Short, regular reading at home continues to build fluency and comprehension"

### Below Standard

**Achievement language patterns**:
- "[Name] is working towards the expected standard in..."
- "[Name] can... with support, and is building independence"
- "[Name] has shown progress in..." (even small progress is progress)

**Growth language patterns**:
- "We are focusing on... in class, with [specific support provided]"
- "[Name]'s next step is to..."
- "A key focus for [Name] this semester has been..."

**Home strategies**:
- "Short, daily practice of [specific skill] at home will complement the support we're providing at school"
- "Reading together for 10 minutes each night — take turns reading pages and talk about what's happening"
- "Praise [Name]'s effort and persistence — building confidence is as important as building skills"

### Well Below Standard

**Achievement language patterns**:
- "[Name] is working on an individualised learning program in..."
- "[Name] is developing foundational skills in..."
- "With targeted support, [Name] is learning to..."

**Growth language patterns**:
- "[Name]'s individual learning goals focus on..."
- "We are working closely with [Name] and your family to support..."
- "The adjustments in [Name]'s [IEP/ILP] are supporting progress in..."

**Home strategies**:
- "Consistent routines at home support [Name]'s learning — a regular time and place for reading together makes a difference"
- "Celebrate small successes — each step forward builds [Name]'s confidence as a learner"
- "[Specific strategy from the IEP/ILP] can be practiced at home through..."

## Subject-Specific Considerations

### English — Reading and Viewing
- Reference: comprehension strategies, text types, reading behaviours
- Good: "[Name] can identify the main idea in a text and locate supporting details"
- Avoid: "Reading is good" (says nothing)

### English — Writing
- Reference: text structure, language features, spelling, handwriting/typing, authorial choices
- Good: "[Name] writes persuasive texts with a clear introduction, supporting arguments, and a conclusion"
- Avoid: "Spelling needs work" (too vague — which patterns? what's the next step?)

### English — Speaking and Listening
- Reference: oral presentations, group discussions, active listening, questioning
- Good: "[Name] contributes relevant ideas in group discussions and builds on others' contributions"
- Avoid: "[Name] talks too much" (judgement, not observation)

### Mathematics (Version 2.0)
- Reference: Number, Algebra, Measurement, Space, Statistics, Probability — but as one holistic comment
- Good: "[Name] can solve problems involving multiplication and division and explain their strategy"
- Avoid: Separate strand-by-strand mini-comments (not the v2.0 reporting approach)

## General Language Principles

### Do
- Use the student's name
- Be specific ("can write compound sentences using 'because' and 'although'" not "writing has improved")
- Reference the curriculum ("at the expected level for Year 3" not "doing well")
- Use active, positive constructions ("is learning to...")
- Mention what the school is doing to support ("we are providing...")

### Don't
- Use negative labels ("lazy," "weak," "poor")
- Compare to siblings or peers
- Use unexplained acronyms (write "Individual Education Plan (IEP)" on first use)
- Make predictions or limit the student ("may never...")
- Raise significant new concerns — these should have been communicated before the report
