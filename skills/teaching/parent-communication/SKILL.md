# Parent Communication (Victorian Schools)

This skill helps VIT-registered teachers write professional, clear, and policy-compliant communications to parents and carers. It covers the full range of written parent communication, aligned to the professional standards in APST 7.

## Before You Begin

Determine the communication type and context:

1. **What kind of communication?** — Quick email, formal letter, newsletter item, interview preparation, complaint response, permission note, or general update?
2. **What's the purpose?** — Inform, invite, respond, reassure, set expectations, or address a concern?
3. **Who is the audience?** — One parent? All parents in the class? The school community?
4. **Is this about a specific student?** — If so, confirm the student's name, preferred pronouns, and any privacy considerations
5. **School type** — Government, Catholic, or independent? (Government schools have specific DET communication policies)

## Workflow

### Step 1: Determine the Communication Type and Tone

| Communication type | Tone | Key considerations |
|---|---|---|
| **Positive update** | Warm, specific, encouraging | Parent loves hearing good news — be genuine, not formulaic |
| **Routine information** | Clear, concise, friendly | Bullet points help; parents scan, they don't read every word |
| **Concern about a student** | Professional, compassionate, collaborative | This is a partnership conversation — "we" not "I" vs "you" |
| **Response to a parent complaint** | Acknowledging, factual, solution-focused | Listen first, defend second. The parent is advocating for their child. |
| **Newsletter contribution** | Engaging, informative, community-building | Parents want to know what their child experiences — show, don't tell |
| **Permission note / consent** | Clear, complete, timely | Parents need to know: what, when, where, cost, risk, how to consent |
| **Parent-teacher interview prep** | Structured, evidence-based, forward-looking | 10–15 minutes goes fast — have a clear structure and specific examples |

### Step 2: Apply Professional Standards

All parent communication should align to APST 7.3. The key professional obligations:

- **Confidentiality**: Never discuss one student with another student's parent
- **Accuracy**: Only communicate what you know to be true; don't speculate
- **Respect**: Address parents as partners in their child's education
- **Inclusivity**: Use plain English; consider translation needs; be culturally aware
- **Policy compliance**: Follow your school's communication policy (especially for complaints and sensitive matters)

### Step 3: Write the Communication

#### Template: Positive Update to a Parent

This is one of the most underused but highest-impact communications. A quick positive note home builds relationships and makes difficult conversations easier later.

```
Subject: Great moment from [Student] today — [brief highlight]

Dear [Parent/Carer name],

I wanted to share a quick positive moment from [student name] today.

[1–2 sentences describing a specific, genuine positive — an action, a comment, a piece of work, an act of kindness.]

It's a pleasure having [student name] in [class/subject].

Warm regards,
[Teacher name]
[Role, School]
```

Keep it short. Keep it genuine. Don't ask for anything. This is pure relationship-building.

#### Template: Raising a Concern

When you need to communicate a concern about a student's learning, behaviour, or wellbeing, the communication should open a conversation, not deliver a verdict.

```
Subject: [Student name] — quick check-in about [brief topic]

Dear [Parent/Carer name],

I'm writing about [student name] and something I've noticed recently that I'd like to discuss with you.

**What I'm seeing at school:**
[2–3 sentences of objective observation. Describe what you've noticed, not your interpretation. "I've noticed [student] is finding it hard to get started on writing tasks and often puts their hand up for help multiple times before beginning." Not: "[Student] is lazy and won't do their work."]

**What we're doing at school:**
[1–2 sentences about the support or strategies already in place.]

**I'd value your perspective:**
Have you noticed anything similar at home? I'd love to chat briefly — I'm available [suggest 2–3 specific times] for a phone call, or we can meet at [upcoming opportunity].

I'm confident we can work together to support [student name].

Warm regards,
[Teacher name]
```

Why this structure works: It's observational, not accusatory. It shows the school is already taking action. It invites partnership, not compliance. And it's specific — the parent knows exactly what the concern is.

#### Template: Responding to a Parent Complaint

A parent complaint is an advocacy moment. The parent cares deeply about their child. Respond to the care, not just the complaint.

```
Subject: Re: [Original subject] — thank you for raising this

Dear [Parent/Carer name],

Thank you for raising your concerns about [topic]. I appreciate you bringing this to my attention — it's important that [student name] feels [safe/supported/challenged/understood] at school.

**To address your points:**
- [Factual response to concern 1 — acknowledge, clarify, or explain]
- [Factual response to concern 2]

**Next steps:**
[What will you do? E.g., "I'll check in with [student] tomorrow about [specific thing]." Or: "I'd like to discuss this further in person — are you available for a meeting on [date options]?"]

Please don't hesitate to contact me if you have further concerns.

Warm regards,
[Teacher name]
```

Important: If the complaint is serious (safety concern, allegation against staff, potential legal issue), do not handle it by email. Acknowledge receipt, then escalate to your Principal or leadership team. Do not make commitments or admissions in writing.

#### Template: Newsletter Contribution

Classroom newsletter items should help parents see what their child experiences:

```
## [Year Level] Update — [Subject/Topic]

This [fortnight/month], [year level] students have been exploring [topic]. Here's a glimpse into what we've been learning:

**What we did:**
[2–3 sentences about the learning activities — use active, engaging language]

**What we learned:**
[1–2 sentences connecting to the curriculum — this helps parents understand the "why" behind the activity]

**Student highlight:**
"[Quote from a student about the learning]" — [Student name], Year [X]

**Looking ahead:**
Next [week/fortnight], we'll be [brief preview].

**How you can support at home:**
[1 practical, free suggestion — e.g., "Ask your child to explain how they solved the maths problem today. The process matters more than the answer!"]

[Teacher name]
[Year Level] Teacher
```

#### Template: Parent-Teacher Interview Preparation

Spend 5 minutes preparing for each interview. A structure keeps the 10–15 minutes focused:

```markdown
## Parent-Teacher Interview Notes — [Student Name]

### Opening (1 min)
- Welcome, thank them for coming
- "The purpose of today's conversation is to share how [student] is progressing and discuss how we can best support their learning together."

### Strengths (2–3 mins)
- [Specific strength 1, with example]
- [Specific strength 2, with example]

### Progress (3–4 mins)
- [Learning area 1]: [Progress statement, evidence, work sample if available]
- [Learning area 2]: [Progress statement, evidence]

### Area for development (3–4 mins)
- [Specific area]: [What we're seeing, what we're doing at school, what would help at home]
- Frame as: "[Student] is working on... One thing that would help is..."

### Parent input (2–3 mins)
- "What are you seeing at home?"
- "Is there anything you'd like us to know or focus on?"

### Closing (1 min)
- Summarise 1–2 agreed actions
- "Thank you for your partnership — please contact me if anything comes up."
```

### Step 4: Quality Check

Before sending any parent communication, review:

1. **Tone check**: Would I be comfortable with my Principal reading this?
2. **Privacy check**: Have I mentioned another student? (Never do this)
3. **Plain English check**: Can a parent who isn't an educator understand every sentence?
4. **Commitment check**: Have I promised something I can't deliver? (Avoid "I'll make sure..." unless you truly can)
5. **Escalation check**: Should this communication come from leadership instead of me?
6. **Record check**: Should I file a copy of this communication? (For complaints, complaints about staff, serious concerns — yes, save a copy)

## Important Principles

### Why proactive positive communication matters
If the first time a parent hears from you is a problem, you're starting every difficult conversation from a deficit. A quick positive note in Week 2 makes the Week 8 concern conversation entirely different — the parent already knows you see their child as more than a problem.

### Why "we" not "you"
When raising concerns: "We're working on..." not "Your child isn't...". The parent is your partner, not your audience. Language that positions you as a team is more effective and more accurate — you are on the same side.

### Why plain English in parent communication
Victorian school communities are diverse. Many parents speak English as an additional language. Many had negative school experiences themselves. Educational jargon ("metacognitive strategies," "phonological awareness," "multiplicative thinking") excludes parents from understanding their own child's education. If you must use a technical term, explain it in the same sentence.

### Why document difficult communications
If a conversation or email exchange with a parent is difficult, contested, or could escalate, keep a record. Brief, factual notes: date, who was present, what was discussed, what was agreed. This is not about being defensive — it's about professional accountability. APST 7.1 and 7.2 require it.

### Handling complaints — the first response is the most important
A parent who complains and feels heard may become your strongest ally. A parent who complains and feels dismissed will escalate. Your first response should acknowledge, not defend. Thank them for raising the concern. Show you've understood. Then, and only then, address the substance.
