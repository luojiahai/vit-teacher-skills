# VIT Inquiry — Provisional to Full Registration

This skill helps provisionally registered teachers (PRTs) navigate the VIT Inquiry process to achieve full registration. The Inquiry is based on Professor Helen Timperley's model of teacher inquiry and knowledge building, and requires demonstrating all 37 APST descriptors at the Proficient level.

## Before You Begin

Understand where the teacher is in their journey:

1. **Registration status** — Are they provisionally registered? How long have they been teaching?
2. **Teaching context** — School type, year levels, subjects, class composition
3. **Days of teaching** — Have they met the 80-day minimum? (Required before applying)
4. **Workplace support** — Do they have a mentor? Has their school established a Workplace Recommendation Panel?
5. **Stage** — Where are they in the 5-stage process? Starting from scratch, or mid-Inquiry?

If the teacher hasn't started yet, begin with Stage 1. If they're partway through, establish which stage and what's already been completed.

Read `shared-references/apst-standards.md` for the full standards framework and `shared-references/vit-policies.md` for VIT requirements. Also read `references/inquiry-5-stages.md` for detailed stage-by-stage guidance.

## The 5 Stages

### Stage 1: Establish Content and Context

**Purpose**: Understand your workplace, your learners, and the learning program that will be the focus of your Inquiry.

**What to document**:

```markdown
## Stage 1: Content and Context

### Education Setting Context
- School name, type (government/Catholic/independent), location
- School demographics (brief — socioeconomic, cultural, linguistic profile)
- School priorities (from the Annual Implementation Plan or Strategic Plan)
- Your role (year level/s, subjects, any additional responsibilities)

### Cohort of Learners
- Class/group description (year level, size, subject)
- Key characteristics: prior learning data, diversity (EAL, additional needs, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students), engagement patterns
- What does this group need most in their learning right now?

### Focus Learners (3–4 learners)
Select 3–4 focus learners who represent the diversity of your cohort. For each:
- Name or identifier (pseudonym for privacy)
- Year level
- Key characteristics relevant to the Inquiry (learning needs, strengths, EAL, additional needs)
- Why you selected this learner

Guidance: Choose learners where you can make a visible difference through your Inquiry focus. Include at least one learner who is working below the expected level and one from an identified priority cohort (EAL, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander, disability/additional needs, or Out-of-Home Care).

### Program of Learning
- Learning area and topic
- Victorian Curriculum achievement standard(s) or VCE Outcome(s) addressed
- Content descriptions
- How the program incorporates: literacy, numeracy, ICT, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander perspectives (these must be addressed across the Inquiry)
- Timeline (typically 4–6 weeks if seeing learners at least twice per week)
```

**Key decisions**: Your choice of focus learners and the program of learning shape everything that follows. Choose a program where you can clearly see learner progress — this makes Stage 5 evaluation much stronger.

### Stage 2: Develop Inquiry Question and Professional Learning

**Purpose**: Frame your Inquiry as a professional learning journey, not just a documentation exercise.

**The Inquiry Question**:

Use VIT's SMART tool: **S**pecific, **M**easureable, **A**chievable, **R**elevant, **T**ime-bound.

A strong inquiry question has this pattern:
> "If I [teaching strategy / intervention], will [focus learners] improve in [specific aspect] as measured by [evidence]?"

| Weak question | Strong question |
|---|---|
| "How can I improve student writing?" | "If I implement explicit sentence-level writing instruction using mentor texts, will my focus learners improve their ability to construct complex sentences in persuasive writing, as measured by pre- and post-writing samples assessed against the Level 6 Writing achievement standard?" |

**Professional Learning**:

Document at least 2 professional learning activities that inform your Inquiry. These should be genuine — things you learned that changed your teaching. Examples:
- Professional reading with notes on how it influenced your approach
- Observation of an experienced colleague (minimum 1 required) — what did you see, what did you learn?
- Workshop, webinar, or PL session relevant to your Inquiry
- Collegiate discussion with your mentor or team

**Professional Conversations** (minimum required):
- At least **2 discussions** about your Inquiry (with mentor, colleague, or PLT)
- At least **1 discussion** specifically about **child safety and professional boundaries**

For each professional conversation, document:
- Date, who participated
- What was discussed
- What you learned or changed as a result

### Stage 3: Develop the Action Plan

**Purpose**: Plan the teaching sequence that will be the core of your Inquiry.

```markdown
## Stage 3: Action Plan

### Inquiry Question
[Your SMART inquiry question from Stage 2]

### Learning Goals
For the cohort overall:
- [Goal aligned to achievement standard]

For each focus learner:
- [Learner 1]: [Specific goal based on their needs and the achievement standard]
- [Learner 2]: [Specific goal]
- [Learner 3]: [Specific goal]
- [Learner 4]: [Specific goal]

### Teaching Strategies
What will you do differently as a result of your professional learning?
- [Strategy 1 — be specific: not "use explicit teaching" but "use 'I Do / We Do / You Do' with worked examples for each new concept, checking for understanding with mini whiteboards before moving to guided practice"]
- [Strategy 2]
- [Strategy 3]

### Resources
- [Resources needed — worksheets, texts, technology, materials]

### Assessment Plan
- **Pre-assessment**: [How will you establish baseline levels? What tool/task?]
- **Formative**: [How will you check progress during the sequence?]
- **Summative**: [How will you measure growth at the end?]
- **Assessment for each focus learner**: [What specific evidence will you collect for each?]

### Inclusive Practice
- How does your plan address the needs of all learners?
- Specific adjustments for focus learners
- EAL strategies (if applicable)
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander perspectives (must be addressed)

### Observation Schedule
- Observation 1: [Date, observer, focus]
- Observation 2: [Date, observer, focus]
- Observation 3: [Date, observer, focus]
Minimum 3 observations of your practice by mentor/experienced colleague are required.
```

### Stage 4: Implement the Action Plan

**Purpose**: Teach the sequence, gather evidence, and document the process.

This is the "doing" stage. You teach the planned sequence, your mentor observes you at least 3 times, and you collect evidence as you go. Document:

- What you taught (lesson plans, resources)
- What students did (work samples — collect these!)
- What your mentor observed (observation notes and feedback)
- What you adjusted (any mid-sequence changes and why you made them)
- Photographs, video, or audio of learning (with appropriate consent)

**Evidence collection tip**: Collect more than you think you need. It's easier to be selective later than to realise you're missing something. Annotate work samples as you go — write directly on them: "This shows [student] can...", "After my feedback, [student] improved by..."

### Stage 5: Evaluate Effectiveness

**Purpose**: Analyse the evidence and reflect on what it shows about your practice and your learners' growth.

```markdown
## Stage 5: Evaluation

### Impact on Focus Learners
For each focus learner:
- **Starting point** (pre-assessment evidence)
- **End point** (summative assessment evidence)
- **Growth demonstrated**: [Specific evidence of what changed]
- **What made the difference**: [Which teaching strategies contributed most?]
- **Next steps**: [What does this learner need next?]

### Impact on Broader Cohort
- [Summary of cohort growth — reference data, work samples]
- [Patterns — what worked for most learners? What didn't?]

### Impact on My Practice
- What have I learned about my teaching?
- What will I continue doing? What will I change?
- How has this Inquiry shifted my practice from Graduate to Proficient level?
- Which APST descriptors are most strongly evidenced by this Inquiry?

### APST Evidence Map
Map your evidence across all 37 descriptors:
| APST Descriptor | Evidence from this Inquiry | Other evidence |
|---|---|---|
| 1.1 | [How your Inquiry shows you know your learners] | [Any additional evidence from broader practice] |
| 1.2 | [...] | [...] |
| ... | ... | ... |
| 7.4 | [...] | [...] |

Note: Not every piece of evidence needs to come from the Inquiry. You can reference your broader teaching practice. But the Inquiry should be the spine holding the evidence together.

### Professional Responsibilities Report
A separate, required statement covering:
- Understanding of legal obligations (mandatory reporting, duty of care, child safety)
- Understanding of professional boundaries and ethics
- How you maintain a child-safe environment
- Reference to the Codes of Conduct and Ethics
```

### The Workplace Recommendation Panel

Once your Inquiry is complete:

1. **Initiate your application** via MyVIT (no more than 30 days before the panel meeting)
2. **Present your Inquiry** to the Workplace Recommendation Panel:
   - Chair: Principal or delegate
   - A mentor-trained teacher
   - A colleague who knows your work
3. **The panel** reviews your evidence against all 37 APST descriptors and makes a recommendation to VIT
4. **VIT** assesses the recommendation and grants full registration

**Preparing for the panel**:
- Have your evidence organised by APST descriptor (not chronologically)
- Be ready to talk about your focus learners and their growth
- Be prepared to discuss what you learned, not just what you did
- The panel is not an exam — it's a professional conversation. They want you to succeed.

### Retaining Evidence

Keep all Inquiry evidence for at least **2 years**. VIT can audit your full registration application. Treat your evidence as an audit-ready record.

## Important Principles

### Why this is not a university assignment
The Inquiry is about demonstrating your practice, not writing a thesis. Provide enough information to understand your Inquiry and evidence — nothing more. VIT panels have limited time. Quality evidence, clearly presented, beats volume.

### Why the focus learners matter
Your focus learners are the evidence that your teaching made a difference. Choose learners where you can show growth. If your focus learners don't improve, the Inquiry can't demonstrate proficiency — so choose a focus where you're confident your improved practice (from your professional learning) will have impact.

### Why one piece of evidence can cover multiple standards
A single annotated lesson plan might evidence APST 1.5 (differentiation), 2.1 (content knowledge), 2.3 (curriculum), 3.2 (planning and sequencing), and 4.1 (participation). You do NOT need 37 separate pieces of evidence. You need comprehensive evidence that collectively covers all 37 descriptors. Annotation is how you show which descriptors each piece covers.

### Why the Inquiry is about growth — yours and theirs
The Inquiry is designed to develop your practice from Graduate to Proficient. The panel wants to see that you've learned something and that your learning improved outcomes for students. An Inquiry where your teaching didn't change much and students didn't progress much won't demonstrate proficiency.

### Time management
A typical Inquiry takes 4–6 weeks of teaching (if seeing learners at least twice per week), but the documentation takes additional time. Start early. Don't leave the write-up to the last week. Collect evidence as you teach — annotating a work sample the day it's produced takes 2 minutes; trying to annotate 20 work samples from memory a month later is painfully slow.
