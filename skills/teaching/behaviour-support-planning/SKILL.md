# Behaviour Support Planning (Victorian Schools)

This skill helps VIT-registered teachers create proactive Behaviour Support Plans (BSPs) and conduct Functional Behaviour Assessments (FBAs) for Victorian students. It follows the School-Wide Positive Behaviour Support (SWPBS) framework endorsed by the Victorian Department of Education.

## Before You Begin

Clarify the scope:

1. **What behaviours are causing concern?** Be specific — observable behaviours, not labels
2. **Is this a Tier 1, 2, or 3 intervention?** (see below)
3. **What data already exists?** Incident reports, observations, ABC charts, previous plans?
4. **Who needs to be involved?** Student, parents/carers, wellbeing team, specialists?
5. **Is there a known diagnosis or NCCD category?** (This informs but does not define the plan)

If behaviour incidents have been occurring, the teacher should document them first using `incident-documentation`. A BSP is built on data, not impressions.

Read `references/swpbs-framework.md` for the SWPBS framework and `references/functional-behaviour-assessment.md` for FBA methodology.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Understand the Tiered Framework

Victorian schools use a three-tiered approach:

| Tier | Who | What |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 (Universal)** | All students | Whole-school expectations, Positive Classroom Management Strategies, consistent routines |
| **Tier 2 (Targeted)** | Students needing additional support (~10–15%) | Small group interventions, check-in/check-out, social skills groups, behaviour contracts |
| **Tier 3 (Intensive)** | Students with significant needs (~1–5%) | Individual BSP, functional behaviour assessment, wraparound supports, specialist involvement |

A BSP is typically a Tier 3 document. Tier 2 interventions may use a simpler behaviour contract or check-in/check-out card. Ask the teacher which tier is appropriate before designing the plan.

### Step 2: Gather Data — Understand Before Planning

Before designing strategies, understand the behaviour. Gather:

- **Incident data**: Read past incident reports (suggest using `incident-documentation` if not yet written)
- **ABC data**: Antecedent (what happened before?), Behaviour (what did the student do?), Consequence (what happened after?)
- **Frequency/duration/intensity**: How often? How long? How severe?
- **Settings**: Where and when does it occur? (Specific class? Time of day? With particular peers?)
- **Strengths**: What does the student do well? When does the behaviour NOT occur?
- **Student voice**: What does the student say about what's happening for them?

### Step 3: Conduct a Functional Behaviour Assessment

All behaviour communicates. The FBA asks: **what is the function of this behaviour?**

Behaviour typically serves one of these functions (sometimes more than one):
- **To get/obtain**: attention, a desired item/activity, sensory stimulation
- **To avoid/escape**: a difficult task, a social situation, sensory discomfort, an unwanted demand

Understanding the function matters because the same behaviour with different functions needs different strategies:
- A student who yells out to **get attention** needs to learn an appropriate way to gain attention
- A student who yells out to **escape a too-hard task** needs task adjustment and a break system

Yelling out is the same behaviour. The function is different. The plan must address the function, not just the surface behaviour.

```markdown
## Functional Behaviour Assessment Summary

### Target Behaviour
[Describe the behaviour in observable, measurable terms. Not "aggressive" but "hits peers with an open hand when frustrated." Not "disruptive" but "calls out comments during teacher instruction, approximately 5–8 times per 60-minute lesson."]

### Data Summary
- Frequency: [e.g., 3–4 times per day, most often between 11am–1pm]
- Duration: [e.g., Episodes last 5–15 minutes]
- Intensity: [e.g., Moderate — does not cause injury but disrupts learning for others]
- Settings: [e.g., Occurs in Mathematics and English, not in specialist classes]

### ABC Pattern
| Antecedent (before) | Behaviour | Consequence (after) |
|---|---|---|
| [Teacher gives independent writing task] | [Student puts head down, refuses to write] | [Teacher approaches, provides 1:1 support] |
| [Peer disagrees during group work] | [Student shouts, pushes materials off desk] | [Student is removed from group, works alone] |
| [Transition from recess to classroom] | [Student enters room loudly, walks around] | [Teacher verbally redirects multiple times] |

### Hypothesis — Function of Behaviour
[Student]'s behaviour appears to serve the function of **escaping/avoiding** [difficult written tasks / peer conflict / unstructured transitions]. When [student] engages in [behaviour], they successfully escape the aversive situation, which reinforces the behaviour.

### Replacement Behaviour
Instead of [target behaviour], [student] will be taught to: [replacement behaviour that serves the same function appropriately]
- e.g., Instead of refusing to write and putting head down → Student will use the "I need help" card to request teacher support
- e.g., Instead of shouting and pushing materials → Student will use the "I need a break" signal (touching a card on desk) and move to the calm corner for 3 minutes
```

### Step 4: Design the Behaviour Support Plan

A BSP has three strategy types:

1. **Proactive strategies** — Prevent the behaviour by changing the environment or antecedents
2. **Teaching strategies** — Teach the replacement behaviour the student needs to use instead
3. **Consequence strategies** — Respond consistently when the behaviour occurs (and when the replacement behaviour is used)

```markdown
# Behaviour Support Plan

**Student:** [Name]
**Year Level:** [Year]
**Date:** [Date]
**Review Date:** [Date — typically 4–6 weeks]
**Plan developed by:** [Names and roles]

## 1. Student Strengths
[List 3–5 genuine strengths. This is not padding — strengths are the foundation you build on.]
- [e.g., Responds positively to 1:1 attention from adults]
- [e.g., Strong oral language skills — can articulate needs when calm]
- [e.g., Enjoys hands-on activities and helping with classroom jobs]

## 2. Target Behaviour
[Observable, measurable description — same as in the FBA]

## 3. Function of Behaviour
[Summary from the FBA — what is the student communicating through this behaviour?]

## 4. Replacement Behaviour
[What will the student do instead? This must serve the same function.]

## 5. Proactive Strategies (Prevention)
These changes reduce the likelihood of the behaviour occurring:
- [e.g., Provide a visual schedule so [student] knows what's coming — reduce transition anxiety]
- [e.g., Pre-teach challenging tasks in a 1:1 session before the whole-class lesson]
- [e.g., Seat [student] near the teacher during independent work for quick check-ins]
- [e.g., Modify writing tasks — [student] can dictate responses or use speech-to-text]

## 6. Teaching Strategies (Skill Building)
These teach the replacement behaviour:
- [e.g., Teach [student] the "I need help" procedure: (1) Put card on desk, (2) Continue with the part you can do, (3) Teacher will come to you within 2 minutes]
- [e.g., Social skills group — weekly session focusing on peer conflict resolution]
- [e.g., Zones of Regulation — teach [student] to identify their emotional state and use appropriate strategies for each zone]
- [e.g., Role-play requesting a break with the wellbeing coordinator, 2× per week]

## 7. Consequence Strategies (Responding)
### When the target behaviour occurs:
1. [e.g., Calm, brief redirection: "[Student], [reminder of expectation]." Do not engage in extended discussion.]
2. [e.g., If behaviour continues: offer the replacement behaviour — "You can use your break card if you need a moment."]
3. [e.g., If behaviour escalates: follow the school's escalation protocol. Document in CASES21.]

### When the replacement behaviour is used:
1. [e.g., Specific praise: "[Student], I noticed you used your help card. That's great problem-solving. Let's look at this question together."]
2. [e.g., Record use of replacement behaviour — track to see if it's increasing over time]

## 8. Data Collection
[How will we track whether the plan is working?]
- [e.g., Daily behaviour chart — teacher rates 3 target behaviours at end of each session]
- [e.g., Weekly frequency count of target behaviour vs replacement behaviour]
- [e.g., Fortnightly review with wellbeing coordinator]

## 9. Communication
- **With student:** [e.g., Daily check-in at 9am, 2-minute reflection at 2:45pm]
- **With parents/carers:** [e.g., Weekly email update, fortnightly phone call]
- **With team:** [e.g., Plan shared with all classroom teachers, specialist teachers, and ES staff via Compass]
- **SSG meeting:** [Date of next Student Support Group meeting]

## 10. Review
- **Fortnightly check:** Is the plan being implemented consistently?
- **4-week review:** Is the target behaviour decreasing? Is the replacement behaviour increasing?
- **6-week review:** Full review — continue, modify, or phase out?
```

### Step 5: Plan the Review and Fade-Out

A good BSP is not forever. It's a scaffold that should fade as the student builds skills. Build in:

- **Short-term review (2–4 weeks)**: Is implementation consistent? Is there any early change?
- **Medium-term review (6–8 weeks)**: Is the behaviour decreasing? What does the data show?
- **Fade-out plan**: How will supports be gradually reduced as the student becomes independent?
- **Escalation triggers**: If the plan isn't working after a reasonable trial, at what point do you seek additional support (Student Support Services, psychologist, paediatrician)?

## Important Principles

### Why function matters more than form
Two students can display the same behaviour for completely different reasons. If you don't understand the function, your intervention might make things worse. A student avoiding a task who is given detention (time away from the task) has just had their avoidance reinforced. The behaviour will increase.

### Why teach a replacement, not just suppress
"Stop hitting" is not a plan. What should the student do instead when they feel frustrated? If you don't teach and reinforce a replacement behaviour, the old behaviour will return — it was meeting a need, and that need still exists.

### Why proactive over reactive
The best behaviour plan means the target behaviour rarely occurs because the environment has been adjusted, the student has been taught new skills, and the triggers have been addressed. A plan that's all consequences ("Step 1: warning, Step 2: time-out, Step 3: principal") is not a behaviour support plan — it's a discipline flowchart. Discipline has its place, but it doesn't teach.

### Why strengths are not optional
Student strengths are the foundation. If a student responds well to 1:1 attention, build that into the proactive strategies (scheduled check-ins). If a student has strong oral skills, use that for replacement behaviours (verbalising needs rather than acting out). A BSP that only lists deficits is demoralising for the student and less effective for the teacher.

### Collaborate with the student
Students, especially older ones, often know what triggers their behaviour and what helps. Ask them. A BSP created without the student is done *to* them; a BSP created with the student is done *with* them. The second one works better.
