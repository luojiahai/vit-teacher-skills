# School-Wide Positive Behaviour Support (SWPBS)

## Overview

SWPBS is the Victorian Department of Education's endorsed framework for promoting positive behaviour and preventing behavioural issues in schools. It is a whole-school, evidence-based approach that emphasises teaching expected behaviours, reinforcing positive behaviours, and using data to guide decision-making.

Source: Department of Education Victoria — Behaviour — Students policy (updated November 2025)

## Core Principles

1. **Behaviour is learned** — Just like academic skills, social and behavioural skills need to be explicitly taught
2. **Prevention over reaction** — Invest in proactive strategies to reduce the need for reactive discipline
3. **Data-driven** — Use behaviour data to identify patterns, target interventions, and monitor progress
4. **Systems approach** — Consistent practices across all staff and settings
5. **Positive reinforcement** — Acknowledge and reinforce the behaviours you want to see

## The Three-Tiered Framework

### Tier 1: Universal (All Students — ~80%)

Foundation level. All students receive:
- **Clearly defined school-wide expectations** (typically 3–5 positively stated expectations, e.g., "Be Safe, Be Respectful, Be a Learner")
- **Explicit teaching of expected behaviours** across all school settings (classroom, yard, canteen, online)
- **Positive reinforcement systems** — specific, contingent praise; token/acknowledgement systems; class-wide reinforcement
- **Consistent responses to behaviour errors** — reteaching, not just punishing
- **Positive Classroom Management Strategies** — routines, active supervision, engagement strategies

When done well, Tier 1 supports prevent most behaviour issues before they start.

### Tier 2: Targeted (~10–15%)

For students who need additional support beyond Tier 1. Typically:
- **Check-In/Check-Out (CICO)** — Daily check-in with a trusted adult, goal setting, feedback throughout the day, check-out with review
- **Small group social skills instruction** — Targeted teaching of specific skills (e.g., conflict resolution, emotional regulation)
- **Behaviour contracts** — Written agreements specifying expected behaviours, reinforcers, and consequences
- **Mentoring** — Regular 1:1 with a staff mentor
- **Break/Reset systems** — Structured opportunities to self-regulate

Tier 2 interventions are typically **short-term** (6–12 weeks), **data-tracked**, and **faded** as skills develop.

### Tier 3: Intensive (~1–5%)

For students with significant, chronic behavioural needs. Tier 3 is individualised:
- **Functional Behaviour Assessment (FBA)** — Systematic analysis of the function of behaviour
- **Individual Behaviour Support Plan (BSP)** — Comprehensive, individualised plan
- **Wraparound supports** — Coordination with external services (psychology, social work, child and adolescent mental health)
- **Frequent data collection** — Daily or session-by-session tracking
- **Regular team meetings** — SSG meetings, care team coordination

Tier 3 is where the `behaviour-support-planning` skill primarily operates.

## Key SWPBS Practices

### Teaching Expected Behaviours

Just as you teach students how to solve an equation, you teach them how to line up, how to ask for help, how to disagree respectfully, and how to transition between activities. A behaviour matrix specifies:

- The expectation (e.g., "Be Respectful")
- What it looks like in each setting (e.g., Classroom: "Use active listening when others are speaking"; Yard: "Include others in games")

### Positive Reinforcement

The ratio of positive to corrective feedback should be at least **4:1**. This isn't about being "soft" — it's about shaping behaviour. Students are more likely to repeat behaviours that are acknowledged. Specific, contingent praise ("You picked up your book and started reading without being asked. That shows great initiative.") is more effective than general praise ("Good job.").

### Data-Based Decision Making

Behaviour data (incident reports, observations, CICO data) should drive decisions:
- **Who** needs additional support? (Look at frequency of office discipline referrals)
- **When** are problems occurring? (Time of day, specific classes, transitions)
- **Where** are problems occurring? (Specific settings)
- **What** behaviours are most common? (Patterns across students)
- **Is it working?** (Track behaviour data over time to evaluate interventions)

### Consistent Responses

All staff should respond to behaviour in consistent ways:
- **Minor behaviours** (calling out, off-task, minor non-compliance) → Classroom-managed: reteach, redirect, brief consequence
- **Major behaviours** (aggression, threats, serious disruption) → Office-managed: documented, consistent consequence, parent contact, return plan

## The Role of Consequences

SWPBS does not mean "no consequences." It means consequences should be:
- **Predictable** — students know what will happen
- **Proportional** — matches the severity
- **Instructional** — teaches a better way, not just punishes
- **Relationship-preserving** — maintains the student's dignity and the teacher-student relationship

## SWPBS and the 2025 Victorian Context

The Department of Education released the "Respectful, Safe, Engaged: Shared Expectations" statement in November 2025, reinforcing:
- Consistent messaging about behaviour expectations across Victorian government schools
- Parent/carer role in supporting behaviour
- Positive Classroom Management Strategies as the foundation
- Tiered response approach for escalating concerns

All Victorian government schools must update their Statement of Values and School Philosophy to reference this framework.
