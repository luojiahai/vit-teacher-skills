# Functional Behaviour Assessment (FBA)

## Overview

A Functional Behaviour Assessment (FBA) is a systematic process for understanding why a behaviour occurs. It identifies the function (purpose) the behaviour serves for the student, which then guides intervention. An FBA is the foundation of any effective Behaviour Support Plan.

## The Core Question

All behaviour communicates. The FBA asks: **"What is the student getting or avoiding through this behaviour?"**

Behaviour is functional — it works for the student. If it didn't work, they wouldn't do it. Understanding the function is critical because the same surface behaviour can serve different functions for different students, requiring different interventions.

## The ABC Framework

The basic unit of behavioural analysis is the ABC sequence:

| Component | Question | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **A — Antecedent** | What happened immediately before the behaviour? | Teacher announces: "Take out your writing books." |
| **B — Behaviour** | What did the student do? (observable, measurable) | Student puts head on desk, pushes book away, says "I'm not doing it." |
| **C — Consequence** | What happened immediately after? | Teacher approaches, asks "What's wrong?", provides individual support to get started. |

The consequence reveals the likely function: the student received 1:1 teacher attention AND delayed starting the writing task. If this pattern repeats, the function may be **escape from difficult writing tasks** (with a secondary function of **gaining adult attention**).

## The Four Functions of Behaviour

Behaviours typically serve one or more of these functions:

### To GET / OBTAIN

| Sub-function | What the student gets | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Attention** | Peer attention, adult attention (positive or negative) | Calling out during class → peers laugh, teacher responds |
| **Tangible** | Access to a desired item or activity | Grabbing another student's device → gets the iPad |
| **Sensory** | Pleasing sensory input | Rocking, hand-flapping, humming → provides sensory stimulation |

### To ESCAPE / AVOID

| Sub-function | What the student avoids | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Task/Demand** | Difficult, boring, or overwhelming academic tasks | Refusing to write → task is delayed or removed |
| **Social situation** | Peer interaction, group work | Leaving the classroom during group activities → escapes the social demand |
| **Sensory** | Uncomfortable sensory input | Covering ears during loud activities → reduces noise |

## Conducting an FBA

### Step 1: Define the Behaviour

Define the target behaviour in **observable, measurable terms**:

| Poor definition | Good definition |
|---|---|
| "Aggressive" | "Hits peers with an open hand on the arm or back" |
| "Disruptive" | "Calls out comments during teacher instruction, 3–8 times per 30-minute lesson" |
| "Disengaged" | "Puts head on desk and closes eyes for 5+ minutes, not responding to verbal prompts" |

### Step 2: Gather Data

Multiple data sources provide triangulation:

1. **ABC charts** — At least 5–10 observations across different settings/times
2. **Incident reports** — Review past documentation for patterns
3. **Frequency/duration/intensity data** — How often? How long? How severe?
4. **Setting events** — Are there broader factors? (Lack of sleep, hunger, conflict at home, medication changes)
5. **Staff interviews** — What do different teachers observe? When does it happen? When doesn't it?
6. **Student interview** — "What's happening when you [behaviour]? What helps? What doesn't?"
7. **Parent/carer input** — Does this happen at home? Under what conditions?

### Step 3: Analyse Patterns

Look for patterns in:
- **Time**: Morning? After lunch? Specific subjects?
- **Setting**: Classroom? Yard? With specific peers? With specific staff?
- **Task demands**: Writing tasks? Group work? Transitions? Unstructured times?
- **Social context**: After peer conflict? When certain students are nearby? When specific staff are present?
- **Health/state**: When tired? Hungry? After a difficult morning?

### Step 4: Form a Hypothesis

State the hypothesis clearly:

> "When [antecedent], [student] engages in [behaviour] in order to [function]. This is more likely to occur when [setting events]."

Example:
> "When presented with independent writing tasks, Jack puts his head on the desk and refuses to write in order to escape the writing demand. This is more likely to occur in the morning literacy block and on days when he has not eaten breakfast."

### Step 5: Test the Hypothesis

- Does the behaviour reliably occur in the predicted conditions?
- Does providing the function proactively (e.g., giving attention before the behaviour) reduce the behaviour?
- Does removing the function (e.g., ensuring the task cannot be escaped) change the behaviour?

## From FBA to Intervention

The FBA directly informs the Behaviour Support Plan. For each function, the intervention must include:

1. **Proactive strategies** — Change the antecedent so the behaviour is less likely to be triggered
2. **Teach a replacement behaviour** — A new behaviour that serves the SAME function but is appropriate
3. **Change the consequences** — Ensure the target behaviour no longer works, but the replacement behaviour does

| Function | Proactive strategy | Replacement behaviour | Consequence adjustment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Escape from difficult writing tasks | Modify tasks to match ability; pre-teach key vocabulary; offer choice of writing tools | Student uses "I need help" card to request teacher support | Task escape is provided when help card is used, NOT when student refuses to work |
| Gain peer attention | Seat near positive peers; provide structured opportunities for peer interaction during lessons | Student gains peer attention by contributing appropriately to discussions (teacher calls on them when hand is raised) | Peer attention follows appropriate participation; calling out is briefly ignored or redirected |
| Escape sensory overload | Provide noise-cancelling headphones; offer calm corner access; reduce visual clutter | Student uses break signal (coloured card on desk) to request 3-minute calm corner break | Breaks are provided when requested appropriately; leaving without permission results in brief reteaching, not extended break |

## When to Seek Specialist Support

Conducting a full FBA is a specialist skill. Classroom teachers can do basic ABC analysis, but consider referring to a behaviour specialist, psychologist, or Student Support Services when:
- The behaviour is severe (causing injury, significant property damage)
- The behaviour is highly frequent (daily or multiple times per day)
- Simple function-based interventions haven't worked after 4–6 weeks of consistent implementation
- Additional data is needed (standardised behaviour assessments, functional analysis)
- The student has complex needs involving mental health, trauma, or disability
