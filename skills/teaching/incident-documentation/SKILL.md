# Incident Documentation (Victorian Schools)

This skill helps VIT-registered teachers document student incidents, behavioural events, and child safety concerns in Victorian schools. It covers a range of documentation types from everyday behaviour logs to mandatory child protection reports, following Victorian legislation and Department of Education (DET) policy.

**Critical disclaimer**: This skill helps with documentation — the written record of what happened. It does not make decisions about whether to report, who to report to, or what actions to take. Teachers must follow their school's policies, consult their Child Safety Officer or Principal, and comply with legal obligations directly. When in doubt, report.

## Before You Begin

First, determine the type of incident. This determines the documentation framework:

| Incident type | Framework | Urgency |
|---|---|---|
| **Child protection concern** (physical abuse, sexual abuse, significant harm) | Four Critical Actions, mandatory reporting CYFA s.184, Failure to Disclose Crimes Act s.327 | Immediate |
| **Serious behavioural incident** (violence, weapon, threat, serious property damage) | ISOC reporting, CASES21 discipline entry | Report within hours |
| **Restraint or seclusion** | Specific data fields (antecedent, de-escalation, type, staff, post-incident) | Document same day |
| **Moderate behavioural incident** (disruption, non-compliance, conflict) | School behaviour log, CASES21 entry | Document same day |
| **Student wellbeing observation** (mood change, concerning comment, peer issue) | Internal notes, wellbeing referral | Document same day |

If you're unsure of the severity, default to the higher level. It's better to over-document than under-document.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Determine the Documentation Framework

Read the relevant policies (see `shared-references/vit-policies.md` for overview):

**For child protection concerns**: Read `references/mandatory-reporting.md` and `references/four-critical-actions.md`.

**For behavioural incidents**: Read `references/incident-templates.md`.

**Key decision points**:
- Could this involve physical or sexual abuse? → Mandatory reporting pathway
- Is this a sexual offence against a child under 16? → Failure to Disclose (Crimes Act s.327) — all adults
- Does the incident involve restraint or seclusion? → Specific DET reporting fields apply
- Is the incident severe (High/Extreme)? → ISOC reporting within strict timeframes

### Step 2: Gather Objective Facts — Not Interpretations

The most important principle in incident documentation: **record what you observed, not what you inferred**. This is both legally important and professionally sound.

**Objective (good)**:
- "Sarah left the classroom without permission at 11:15am. I observed her walking toward the oval."
- "Tom said: 'Nobody ever listens to me.' He then put his head on the desk and did not respond for approximately 10 minutes."
- "I observed a 3cm bruise on Jamal's right forearm. When I asked how it happened, Jamal said: 'I fell off my bike yesterday.'"

**Subjective (avoid)**:
- "Sarah was angry and stormed out." (You cannot know her emotional state — you observed her leaving.)
- "Tom was being defiant." (Judgement — describe what he said and did, not what you assume he intended.)
- "The bruise looked suspicious." (Opinion — describe what you observed, report the facts, let Child Protection investigate.)

**For student disclosures, record verbatim words where possible**:
- Use quotation marks for the student's exact words
- If you can't recall verbatim, write: "[Student] said something like: '...'" and note it's paraphrased
- Record who else was present when the disclosure was made
- Do not prompt or interview the student — that's not a teacher's role. Listen, document, report.

### Step 3: Write the Incident Report

Use this structure for all incident documentation. Adapt the detail level to the incident severity:

```markdown
# Incident Report

**Date of incident:** [DD/MM/YYYY]
**Time:** [HH:MM]
**Location:** [e.g., Classroom 12, School oval near the gym, Online/Teams]
**Report written by:** [Name, role]
**Date of report:** [DD/MM/YYYY]

## Who Was Involved
- [Name], student, Year [X] — [role in incident: directly involved / witness / impacted]
- [Name], staff member — [role: supervising teacher / witness / first responder]
- [List all students and staff present at the time]

## What Happened
[Objective chronological account. Include:]
- What was happening before the incident (context/antecedent)
- What you observed, heard, or were told — in sequence
- Exact words spoken where relevant (use quotation marks)
- What you did in response
- How the situation was resolved or de-escalated

## Immediate Actions Taken
- [Action 1 — e.g., Separated students; administered first aid; contacted Principal]
- [Action 2 — e.g., Notified parents/carers at 2:30pm by phone]
- [Action 3 — e.g., Recorded in CASES21 as [category]]

## Notifications
| Who | When | How | By whom |
|---|---|---|---|
| Principal/Delegate | [Time/date] | [In person/phone/email] | [Name] |
| Parent/Carer of [student] | [Time/date] | [Phone/email/meeting] | [Name] |
| Child Safety Officer | [Time/date] | [Method] | [Name] |
| DFFH Child Protection | [Time/date] | [Phone — intake number] | [Name] |
| Victoria Police | [Time/date] | [000 / local station] | [Name] |
| ISOC | [Time/date] | [1800 126 126 / eduSafe Plus] | [Name] |

## Witness Statements
### Witness 1: [Name, role]
[Their account, in their words where possible. If they wrote their own statement, note: "See attached written statement."]

### Witness 2: [Name, role]
[Their account]

## Follow-Up Required
- [ ] [Action item — e.g., Check in with student tomorrow morning]
- [ ] [Action item — e.g., Provide written notice to parents within 24 hours]
- [ ] [Action item — e.g., Convene Student Support Group meeting]

## Confidentiality
This record is confidential. Storage: [e.g., Student file — sealed envelope "Confidential — Principal class only" / CASES21 / Internal wellbeing system]

## Signature
[Name], [Role]
Date: [DD/MM/YYYY]
```

### Step 4: Apply the Correct Reporting Pathway

#### Mandatory Reporting to Child Protection (CYFA s.184)

Applies when you form a **belief on reasonable grounds** (more than rumour, less than certainty) that a child has suffered or is likely to suffer significant harm from physical injury or sexual abuse AND the parents have not protected or are unlikely to protect the child.

- **Report to**: DFFH Child Protection (local intake) or Child Protection Crisis Line 13 12 78 (24/7)
- **When**: As soon as practicable after forming the belief
- **Document**: The report date, time, who took the report, reference number
- **Do not**: Investigate yourself, delay, or inform parents if DFFH advises against it

#### Failure to Disclose (Crimes Act s.327)

Applies to **all adults** (not just teachers). If you form a reasonable belief that a sexual offence has been committed against a child under 16, you must report to **Victoria Police**. Failure to disclose is a criminal offence.

#### ISOC Reporting

For incidents that meet the threshold for reporting to the Incident Support and Operations Centre:
- **Extreme severity**: Report **immediately** (within 1 hour) via 1800 126 126
- **High severity**: Report within **3 hours** via eduSafe Plus or 1800 126 126

### Step 5: Handle Confidentiality and Storage

Incident records are confidential. Storage depends on the type:

| Record type | Storage |
|---|---|
| Child protection reports | Student file — sealed envelope marked "Confidential — to be opened by Principal class only" |
| Behaviour incidents | CASES21 or school's student management system |
| General wellbeing notes | School's wellbeing/student management system |
| Restraint/seclusion | Specific DET recording system + student file |
| Staff injury/incident | eduSafe Plus |

## Important Principles

### Why objective, not interpretive
Two reasons. First, legal: incident reports may be read by parents, lawyers, DFFH, Police, or a coroner. Your documentation should stand up to scrutiny. "I observed..." holds up. "He was aggressive..." does not. Second, professional: interpretations can be wrong. The child who "refused to comply" might have been having a trauma response. Document what you saw; let the professionals interpret.

### Why document immediately
Memory degrades quickly. The best incident report is written within an hour of the event. If you can't write the full report immediately, make dot-point notes with times and verbatim quotes while the details are fresh, then expand later.

### Why the Four Critical Actions matter
The Four Critical Actions framework is not optional — it's the DET-mandated process for responding to child abuse. Schools are audited on their compliance. Following the framework protects both the child and you.

### The "who, what, where, when" rule
Every incident report should answer: Who was there? What happened (in sequence)? Where did it happen? When did it happen (exact times matter)? If the report doesn't answer these four questions, it's incomplete.

### When in doubt, report
Teachers sometimes hesitate to make mandatory reports because they're unsure. The legal standard is "reasonable belief," not certainty. If a reasonable teacher in your position would have the same concern, report it. The consequences of not reporting are far worse than the consequences of reporting a concern that turns out to be unfounded.
