# Mandatory Reporting — Victorian Legal Framework

## Overview

Teachers in Victoria have specific legal obligations to report child abuse and safety concerns. These are not optional — they are legal duties with penalties for non-compliance. This reference summarises the key obligations. Always consult your school's Child Safety Officer or Principal for guidance on specific situations.

Sources:
- Children, Youth and Families Act 2005 (Vic) — Part 4.4, Section 184
- Crimes Act 1958 (Vic) — Sections 327 and 49O
- Ministerial Order No. 870 — Child Safe Standards

## The Three Key Legal Obligations

### 1. Mandatory Reporting (CYFA s.184)

**Who it applies to**: Registered teachers, principals, school counsellors, registered medical practitioners, nurses, midwives, police officers, and others in prescribed professions.

**When you must report**: If, in the course of practising your profession, you form a **belief on reasonable grounds** that a child is in need of protection because the child:
- Has suffered, or is likely to suffer, **significant harm as a result of physical injury** AND the child's parents have not protected, or are unlikely to protect, the child from that harm; OR
- Has suffered, or is likely to suffer, **significant harm as a result of sexual abuse** AND the child's parents have not protected, or are unlikely to protect, the child from that harm.

**What "reasonable belief" means**: More than mere rumour or suspicion, but less than certainty or proof. A belief is reasonable if a reasonable person in the same position, with the same information, would form the same belief.

**To whom**: Department of Families, Fairness and Housing (DFFH) Child Protection.

**When**: As soon as practicable after forming the belief.

**Penalty for failure to report**: 10 penalty units (approximately $1,900 as of 2025).

**Important**: The report must be made by the person who formed the belief — it cannot be delegated to the Principal or Child Safety Officer (though you should inform them). You remain legally responsible for making the report.

### 2. Failure to Disclose (Crimes Act s.327)

**Who it applies to**: **All adults** in Victoria (not just professionals). This is broader than mandatory reporting.

**When you must disclose**: If you have information that leads you to form a reasonable belief that a **sexual offence** has been committed against a **child under 16 years of age**, you must disclose that information to Victoria Police **as soon as reasonably possible**.

**Key difference from mandatory reporting**: This applies to ALL adults, covers ALL sexual offences (not just those where parents failed to protect), and requires reporting to Police (not Child Protection).

**Penalty**: Up to 3 years imprisonment.

**Exceptions**: You do not need to disclose if:
- You have a reasonable excuse (e.g., fear for your own safety)
- The information is subject to legal professional privilege
- The victim is now 16+ and requests you not disclose (with some conditions)

### 3. Failure to Protect (Crimes Act s.49O)

**Who it applies to**: People in positions of **authority** within organisations (e.g., principals, school leadership).

**What it requires**: If you know there is a **substantial risk** that a child under 16 under the care of your organisation will become a victim of a **sexual offence** by an adult associated with the organisation, you must take **reasonable steps** to remove or reduce that risk.

**Penalty**: Up to 5 years imprisonment.

**Note for classroom teachers**: This primarily applies to principals and leadership. However, if you become aware of a risk and your leadership does not act, you should escalate through appropriate channels (regional office, Victoria Police, Child Protection).

## The Child Safe Standards (Ministerial Order No. 870)

All Victorian schools must comply with 11 Child Safe Standards. These create a whole-school framework for child safety. Key implications for teachers:

- Standard 5: Procedures for responding to and reporting suspected child abuse
- Standard 6: Strategies to identify and reduce or remove risks of child abuse
- All staff must receive annual training on mandatory reporting and child safety

## Practical Guidance

### If a student discloses abuse to you

1. **Listen** — Do not promise confidentiality. Say: "I need to tell someone who can help keep you safe."
2. **Don't investigate** — Do not question the student beyond what they volunteer. Your role is to listen and report, not to investigate. Questioning can compromise a police investigation.
3. **Document verbatim** — Write down the student's exact words as soon as possible after the disclosure
4. **Report immediately** — Contact your school's Child Safety Officer and/or make the mandatory report yourself
5. **Follow up** — Confirm the report was made. Record the date, time, who took the report, and the reference number.

### What NOT to do

- **Do not** promise confidentiality
- **Do not** question or interview the student
- **Do not** contact parents if DFFH or Police advise against it (this can compromise an investigation or put the child at risk)
- **Do not** discuss the matter with colleagues who don't need to know
- **Do not** delay — report as soon as practicable
- **Do not** assume someone else will report — if you formed the belief, you are responsible for reporting (or confirming it was reported)

### If you're unsure whether to report

The default answer is: **report**. The threshold is "reasonable belief," not certainty. If a reasonable teacher in your position would be concerned, report it. The consequences of not reporting a genuine case of abuse are far more serious than reporting a concern that turns out to be unfounded.

Consult your school's Child Safety Officer or Principal for guidance, but remember: if you formed the belief, you remain the mandated reporter. Delegation is not a defence.
